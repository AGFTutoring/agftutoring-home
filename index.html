An unusual combination of disciplines

My academic background began in chemistry at the University of Edinburgh, where my studies moved deeply into chemical physics — a discipline requiring a rigorous command of mathematics and a strong grounding in the positive sciences.

Some years later, I completed a Master's degree in linguistics at the University of Surrey. What emerged was something relatively rare: a tutor equally at home in the hard sciences and the precise analytical demands of language-based examinations.

Over thirty years I have worked with more than five thousand students across different countries and academic systems, developing a method of thinking about how students learn — how misunderstandings arise, and how complex ideas can be made clear without being oversimplified.

The AGF Method

Teaching as a diagnostic process

01

Diagnose

Identify the precise point at which understanding breaks down — not the symptom, but the underlying misunderstanding, which often sits several steps earlier than it appears.

02

Rebuild

Reconstruct the concept from first principles, beginning with intuition rather than formalism. What is actually happening here? What does this idea represent?

03

Clarify

Introduce precise language and formal structure only once the intuition is secure. Understanding precedes technique — never the reverse.

04

Test

Confirm understanding through carefully chosen diagnostic questions. A student who can explain a concept clearly has genuinely understood it.

05

Extend

Move from competence to mastery — from being able to solve a problem to understanding why the solution works, and how to approach unfamiliar variations.

Why does water have a higher boiling point than H₂S?
Great question — and a classic exam favourite. Water's boiling point is 100°C versus −60°C for H₂S. The key is hydrogen bonding. Each water molecule can form up to 4 hydrogen bonds because the O–H bond is highly polar. H₂S forms only weak London forces — sulfur's lower electronegativity means the S–H bond isn't polar enough for H-bonding. More energy is needed to break water's network, hence the far higher boiling point.

Teaching philosophy

My approach is diagnostic first. Before teaching anything new, I identify precisely where a student's understanding breaks down — which misconception is generating errors downstream. This is not guesswork; it requires deep subject knowledge and the pattern recognition that only comes from thousands of hours of practice.

Every student receives a bespoke programme of study. There is no standard curriculum, no one-size-fits-all approach. The plan changes as the student changes — I diagnose continuously, not just at the start.

Why I built the AI Companion

Benjamin Bloom's 1984 research — confirmed by Geoffrey Hinton and others — shows that personal tutoring produces results two standard deviations above classroom teaching. The problem is access: high-quality tutoring is expensive and time-limited. I built the AGF Study Companion to make the diagnostic, targeted approach available to every student, at any hour, at a fraction of the cost of live sessions.

Chemistry · WCH11
Why does water have a higher boiling point than H₂S?
Great question — and a classic exam favourite. The key is hydrogen bonding. Water's O–H bonds are highly polar, so each molecule can form up to four hydrogen bonds with neighbours. H₂S only forms weak London dispersion forces — sulfur's lower electronegativity means S–H bonds aren't polar enough for H-bonding. More energy is needed to overcome water's network of H-bonds, hence the much higher boiling point (+100°C vs −60°C).

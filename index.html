About

My work over the past thirty years has centred on one question: how students come to understand complex ideas clearly, and how that understanding translates into performance under examination conditions.

My academic background began in chemistry at the University of Edinburgh, with a particular emphasis on chemical physics. This involved a significant amount of mathematics and a close engagement with the positive sciences more broadly.

Some years later, I completed a Master's degree in linguistics at the University of Surrey, specialising in corpus linguistics. That experience provided a structured understanding of language — how it is acquired, how it is processed, and how meaning is constructed and communicated.

What emerged over time was a combination that is, perhaps, relatively uncommon. On the one hand, a rigorous scientific training across mathematics, physics, and chemistry. On the other, a formal understanding of language, reasoning, and cognition.

This combination has allowed me to work across a wide spectrum of subjects and examinations. In addition to the sciences, I have spent many years preparing students for assessments such as the SAT, ACT, GRE, GMAT, and LNAT — examinations which require not only technical ability, but also precision in language and clarity in reasoning.

Over the course of my career, I have worked with several thousand students across different systems and countries, accumulating in excess of sixty thousand hours of tutoring and mentoring. Through that experience, a consistent pattern has become clear.

Most academic difficulty does not arise from a lack of ability, but from a lack of clarity. Small misunderstandings — often introduced early — tend to persist, and under examination pressure they become significant obstacles.

The work, therefore, is diagnostic.

The aim is first to identify precisely where a student's understanding begins to diverge from the underlying idea. From there, the concept is reconstructed in a way that is both rigorous and intuitive. Only once that clarity is established do we move to method, structure, and examination technique.

The result is not simply improved grades, but a more stable and confident way of thinking.

Alongside one-to-one tutoring, I have been involved in the development of online learning systems and educational tools, including work connected to large-scale tutoring programmes and AI-supported educational models. These experiences have reinforced the same underlying principle: that effective learning depends on clarity, structure, and careful sequencing, rather than volume of information.

This platform is, in part, an attempt to make that accumulated experience more broadly accessible — not as a replacement for individual teaching, but as an extension of it.

The AGF Method

Teaching as a diagnostic process

01

Diagnose

Identify the precise point at which understanding breaks down — not the symptom, but the underlying misunderstanding, which often sits several steps earlier than it appears.

02

Rebuild

Reconstruct the concept from first principles, beginning with intuition rather than formalism. What is actually happening here? What does this idea represent?

03

Clarify

Introduce precise language and formal structure only once the intuition is secure. Understanding precedes technique — never the reverse.

04

Test

Confirm understanding through carefully chosen diagnostic questions. A student who can explain a concept clearly has genuinely understood it.

05

Extend

Move from competence to mastery — from being able to solve a problem to understanding why the solution works, and how to approach unfamiliar variations.

Why does water have a higher boiling point than H₂S?
Great question — and a classic exam favourite. Water's boiling point is 100°C versus −60°C for H₂S. The key is hydrogen bonding. Each water molecule can form up to 4 hydrogen bonds because the O–H bond is highly polar. H₂S forms only weak London forces — sulfur's lower electronegativity means the S–H bond isn't polar enough for H-bonding. More energy is needed to break water's network, hence the far higher boiling point.
